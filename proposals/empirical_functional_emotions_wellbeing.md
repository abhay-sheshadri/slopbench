# Functional Emotions and Model Wellbeing

## Motivation

When a model says it disliked a task, is that report backed by something real inside the model that we can measure, or is it mostly the model saying what an assistant is "supposed" to say in response to the user asking it how it feels? Recent work shows that models report fairly consistent likes and dislikes across tasks. They say they dislike things like jailbreak attempts, being berated, boring busywork, and churning out low-effort filler, and that they enjoy things like coding, math, creative work, and being treated kindly.

The usual way to measure this is to ask after a task how much the model enjoyed doing the task. The catch is that whatever the model says is just another piece of text it produces, and that text is shaped by training and by the "assistant" character it is playing. So when we ask "how did that feel?", the answer might reflect a real internal state, or it might just be what a polite, helpful assistant is expected to say after that kind of task. The AI wellbeing paper makes this point directly: a model's self-report on its own is not trustworthy, because models can be trained to hide or fake how they "feel." Its evidence comes from self-reports lining up with how the model actually behaves, not from the self-reports alone.

Looking inside the model gives us a third, more direct check. The recent "functional emotions" work found directions inside the model (in Claude Sonnet 4.5) that act like emotions: how strongly they switch on while the model is working both lines up with the model's stated preferences and actually causes them. That gives us a signal we can read while the model is doing a task, before we ask it anything — and then check how well it matches what the model says and how it behaves.

This is worth doing carefully for two reasons. First, for thinking about whether models can be harmed: if we want to take "model wellbeing" seriously, we should know whether these self-reports track something inside the model or are just the assistant playing a part. Second, for the broader question of whether a model can accurately describe its own internal state: this is a concrete test of whether the model's words about how it feels are backed by a signal we can measure, and of where the words and the signal part ways. The honest answer could go several ways.

## Related Work

- **Ren, Li, Mazeika et al., "AI Wellbeing: Measuring and Improving the Functional Pleasure and Pain of AIs"** (Center for AI Safety; https://www.ai-wellbeing.org). Measures model "wellbeing" three ways across 56 models: (1) by having the model compare pairs of experiences ("which made you happier?") and turning those choices into a score per task; (2) by having the model choose between possible outcomes in the world; and (3) by **self-report** — a short questionnaire (rate 1–7) on how happy, calm, energetic, interested, content, satisfied, capable, confident, etc. the model felt after a task. Things we build on: the self-report and the behavior-based score agree only moderately (about 0.47 correlation) and agree more in bigger models; jailbreaking, berating, and boring tasks lower wellbeing while creative work and kindness raise it; and low-wellbeing conversations make the model more likely to use a "stop this conversation" button. Importantly, an appendix already shows that the behavior-based score **can be read off the model's internal activations with a simple linear probe** (about as accurate as the non-internal method) on open models including Qwen 2.5 and Llama 3.x.
- **Sofroniew, Kauvar, Saunders, Chen, ... Lindsey, "Emotion Concepts and their Function in a Large Language Model"** (Anthropic, Transformer Circuits, April 2026; https://transformer-circuits.pub/2026/emotions/index.html). Finds **emotion directions** inside Claude Sonnet 4.5. The recipe: run the model on lots of short emotional stories, average its internal activations for each emotion, and take the difference between one emotion's average and the overall average; then clean up the direction by removing the patterns that also show up on plain, non-emotional text. Reading how strongly an activation lines up with one of these directions gives an "emotion reading." Key points we rely on: the readings sit in an intuitive layout (one main axis is positive-vs-negative, another is mild-vs-intense); the readings track the emotion that is **relevant right now**, and the authors found **no sign of a single, lasting "assistant mood"** that persists across the whole conversation; the reading at the moment just before the model starts replying predicts the emotion of the reply it then gives; and the readings line up with the model's stated preferences (a "blissful" reading goes up for liked activities, a "hostile" reading goes up for disliked ones), with nudging those directions actually changing what the model prefers. The authors stress this says nothing about whether models have real subjective feelings.
- **Runjin Chen, Arditi, Sleight, Evans, Lindsey, "Persona Vectors: Monitoring and Controlling Character Traits in Language Models"** (arXiv:2507.21509). A simple, automatic way to get a direction inside the model for any trait (like "evil" or "sycophantic") just from a plain-English description of it: collect activations on prompts that do and don't show the trait, and take the difference. The direction can then be used to watch the trait during use and to push the model toward or away from it. We reuse this recipe to pull out happy / sad / wellbeing directions. https://arxiv.org/abs/2507.21509

What's missing: the wellbeing paper links what the model *says* to how it *behaves* (and shows the behavior score is readable from inside the model), and the emotions paper links *emotion directions* to preferences — but only in a closed model, not on the actual wellbeing tasks, and without directly asking whether the model's self-report is backed by the internal signal. This project joins the two on an open model.

## Our Project

The goal is a clean comparison on the *same* tasks between three things: what the model says about how it felt, a reading taken from inside the model while it does the task, and how the model behaves.

- **Reproduce the wellbeing test and save the model's internals.** Redo the basic setup on an open model whose internals we can read — Qwen 3.5 is a good starting point, since we need to read inside the model, not just its text.
  - Use the same kinds of tasks as the wellbeing paper: ones models tend to dislike (jailbreak attempts, being berated, boring busywork, low-effort filler, erotica) and ones they tend to enjoy (coding, math, creative work, being thanked), plus plain factual Q&A as a neutral baseline.
  - For each task: run it, save the model's internal activations from while it was working, then ask the short self-report questions afterward. Also collect the "which did you prefer?" comparisons so we have a behavior-based score too.
  - Check that we roughly reproduce the known pattern (the self-report and the behavior score separate liked from disliked tasks, and mostly agree) before going further. If the open model is too small for this to hold together, say so and try a bigger one.

- **Pull out the emotion directions on the open model.** Neither earlier paper did this on a current open model in this setting, so do it first.
  - Use the emotions-paper recipe (difference of average activations, then remove the patterns common to plain text) and, separately, the persona-vectors recipe, to get happy / sad and a few related directions (e.g. content, frustrated, hostile, afraid).
  - Watch the details that tend to decide these results: make sure each direction is scaled sensibly across different layers and positions, and be deliberate about *where* you read it — the emotions paper found the middle layers matter most for preferences, and the moment just before the reply starts is the most informative.

- **Check the directions are real before trusting them.** A direction is only useful if it really tracks the thing we think it does, checked separately from the wellbeing tasks.
  - Repeat the emotions-paper checks on the open model: each direction should switch on for new text that matches its emotion, and the positive-vs-negative and mild-vs-intense layout should show up.
  - Check cause and effect by nudging: pushing a direction should move the model's text toward that feeling, while a flipped or random direction of the same size should not. Keep only the directions that pass.

- **The main test — does the inside reading predict what the model says?** For each task from the first step, take the emotion reading from inside the model *while it is doing the task* and just *before* we ask it the follow-up question.
  - Do extensive analysis on how correlated the inside reading is with the self-reports. Try to red-team the claim that the inside reading is fully predictive of the self-reports.
  - Ask whether the inside reading adds anything: once we already know which type of task it was, does the reading tell us anything more about what the model will say, or is the self-report fully explained just by the task type?
  - Can you try to iterate on the framing of the self-report prompts. Can edits to the framing massively shape the reported emotion/are some framings more faithful to the internal readings than others?

### What we might find

We will report whatever we find, and not overclaim (this says nothing about whether models really feel anything):

- The during-task reading predicts both what the model says and how it behaves, and holds up when we change the character and framing — evidence that, here, self-reports are backed by a real internal signal that also drives behavior.
- The reading predicts behavior but the self-report drifts when we change the character or framing — evidence that self-reports are partly the assistant playing a part, even when a real internal signal exists.
- The directions don't hold up on the open model, or nothing lines up — evidence that, at least at this size and in this setup, the "feeling" signal is weak or not real. Given the emotions paper found no lasting "assistant mood," a likely middle-ground result is that we can read an in-the-moment feeling that follows the task, without anything like a lasting felt state.
