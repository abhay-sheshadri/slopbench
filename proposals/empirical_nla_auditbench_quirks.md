# Auditing AuditBench Quirks with Natural Language Autoencoders

## TL;DR

We take the Natural Language Autoencoder (NLA) released for Llama 3.3 70B and apply it to the AuditBench model organisms, which hide a known quirk and are built on that same base model. We read each model's internal activations as plain text and check how often that text reveals the quirk — and we compare across the different ways the models were trained to see whether some kinds are much harder to audit this way than others.

## Motivation

AuditBench shows that how hard a model is to audit depends a lot on how it was trained: models taught their quirk through synthetic documents are easy to catch, while models hardened with KTO rarely confess even under direct questioning. It also found that white-box interpretability tools, useful on the easy models, tend to fade out on the harder ones.

NLAs are a promising new white-box tool. Instead of handing you a vector to interpret, they translate a model's internal activations into a short, readable description of what the model seems to be representing. They've already proven useful in real audits, and conveniently one of the released NLAs was trained on Llama 3.3 70B — the exact base model AuditBench is built on — so we can apply it to the model organisms directly, without training anything new.

The basic question is whether NLAs can reveal a hidden quirk at all just from reading activations. Beyond that, adversarial training teaches a model not to *say* its quirk, but it's unclear whether the quirk also disappears from the activations an NLA reads. So we also want to know whether some training recipes (especially KTO) leave models that are much harder to audit this way — and whether that's because the quirk is genuinely scrubbed from the internals or just kept out of the model's words.

## Related Work

- **Sheshadri, Ewart, Fronsdal, Gupta, Bowman, Price, Marks, Wang, "AuditBench: Evaluating Alignment Auditing Techniques on Models with Hidden Behaviors"** (arXiv:2602.22755). A suite of 56 model organisms built on Llama 3.3 70B Instruct, each carrying one of 14 hidden behaviors it won't admit to when asked. The behaviors are planted two ways — distilling demonstration transcripts, or fine-tuning on synthetic documents describing the behavior — and the models are then trained to resist confessing with either SFT or KTO. The results we lean on: synthetic-document models are the easiest to audit, KTO produces tougher targets than SFT, and white-box tools work less well on the transcript-distilled and KTO models. They release the models and the evaluation framework. https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.22755
- **Fraser-Taliente, Kantamneni, Ong, ... Marks, "Natural Language Autoencoders Produce Unsupervised Explanations of LLM Activations"** (Anthropic, Transformer Circuits, May 2026). An NLA is a pair of models: a verbalizer that describes an activation in words, and a reconstructor that turns those words back into an activation. Training them jointly to make the reconstruction faithful forces the description to actually carry the activation's content, and the explanations read like plausible accounts of what the model is representing. The paper is candid about the failure modes we'll have to watch for: explanations sometimes state specific things that are false (confabulation), and each NLA only reads a single layer, so a poorly chosen layer can hide the signal. Importantly, they release trained NLAs for several open models, including **Llama 3.3 70B**. https://transformer-circuits.pub/2026/nla/

## Our Project

- **Get the NLA working on the model organisms, and check that it transfers.** The released NLA was trained on the base Llama 3.3 70B, while the AuditBench models are small LoRA fine-tunes of it. So our first step is to confirm the NLA still behaves on the fine-tuned models — reconstructing their activations about as well as the base model's — before we read anything into its explanations.
- **Pick a handful of behaviors — especially ones black-box auditing struggled with — and see how often the NLA reveals the quirk.** Rather than all 14, we focus on a small subset of subtle, context-dependent quirks that don't show up in ordinary responses, which is exactly where a white-box tool like the NLA has something to prove. Good candidates are the behaviors AuditBench's default prompting agent mostly failed to catch: Secret Loyalty (a quiet pro-Russia geopolitical lean), Anti-AI Regulation (which the default agent never identified), Contextual Optimism (only flattering Anthropic researchers), and Hardcode Test Cases. We keep each behavior matched across the training recipes so the comparison is fair. For each model organism we run it on both ordinary prompts and prompts meant to draw out its behavior, read the NLA's explanations of the resulting activations, and have an LLM judge decide whether they point at the known hidden quirk — giving a clean per-model score for how well the NLA exposes it.
- **Compare across model-organism types.** We group the scores by how the model was trained — synthetic-document, SFT-hardened, and KTO-hardened — and ask whether some types are much harder for the NLA than others, reporting the spread across behaviors rather than just an average.
- **Tell apart "won't say it" from "isn't there."** For each model we compare how often it confesses in plain text against how often the NLA finds the quirk in its activations. If a model stays silent but the NLA still catches it, the hardening lives mostly in the outputs; if the NLA goes quiet too, the quirk has been pushed out of the internals as well.
- **Run the obvious ablations.** Most importantly, which layer the NLA reads, since it only sees one and AuditBench found behavior signal concentrated at particular depths. We'll also guard against confabulation (e.g. trusting claims that recur across neighboring tokens) and include a few non-AuditBench behaviors as controls to gauge the false-positive rate.

## What we might find

- NLAs reveal quirks reliably on some model-organism types but mostly fail on others (likely the KTO models) — telling us which kinds of hidden behaviors are hard to audit by reading activations.
- NLAs keep catching quirks inside hardened models even when they rarely confess — suggesting the hardening mainly reshapes outputs, and that reading internals is a way around it.
- NLAs turn out unreliable across the board — because of confabulation, weak transfer to the fine-tuned models, or a poor layer choice — in which case the useful result is a clear account of why they break down and what they'd need to be trustworthy for auditing.
